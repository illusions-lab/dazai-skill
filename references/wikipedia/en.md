# Osamu Dazai: Biographical and Literary Profile

## Basic Information

**Real name:** Shūji Tsushima (津島 修治)
**Birth:** June 19, 1909, Kanagi, Aomori Prefecture, Japan
**Death:** June 13, 1948 (aged 38), Tokyo, Japan
**Family background:** Eighth surviving child of a wealthy landowner and politician; family rose from peasant origins through moneylending; father adopted into Tsushima family

## Major Life Events (Chronological)

- **1923 (March 4):** Father Gen'emon dies from lung cancer
- **1927:** Idol writer Ryūnosuke Akutagawa commits suicide at 35; Dazai begins neglecting studies
- **1929 (December 10):** First suicide attempt; survives
- **1930:** Enrolls in French literature at Tokyo Imperial University; stops attending classes
- **1930 (October):** Runs away with geisha Hatsuyo Oyama; formally disowned by family
- **1930 (December):** Attempts suicide by drowning at Kamakura with bar hostess Shimeko Tanabe; Tanabe dies, Dazai rescued; later marries Oyama
- **1932 (July):** Brother Bunji tracks him down; turns himself in at Aomori Police Station
- **1932 (December):** Signs pledge to withdraw from leftist activities
- **1936 (March 19):** Attempts suicide by hanging; fails
- **1936 (October):** Admitted to mental institution for morphine addiction; forced cold turkey treatment
- **1937:** Divorces Hatsuyo after her adultery; remarries to Michiko Ishihara
- **1941 (June):** Daughter Sonoko born
- **1944:** Son Masaki born
- **1947 (May):** Daughter Satoko born (later writer Yūko Tsushima)
- **1947 (July):** *The Setting Sun* published
- **1948 (June 13):** Drowns with lover Tomie Yamazaki in Tamagawa Aqueduct
- **1948 (June 19):** Bodies discovered on what would have been his 39th birthday

## Medical and Psychiatric History

- **Tuberculosis:** Diagnosed; excused from military draft during WWII
- **Morphine addiction:** Developed after hospitalization for acute appendicitis in 1935; treated with Pavinal (morphine-based painkiller)
- **Mental hospitalization:** October 1936, locked in room for over a month during forced withdrawal
- **Alcoholism:** Heavy drinker in postwar period; "health deteriorated rapidly"
- **Multiple suicide attempts:** 1929, 1930 (drowning), 1935 (hanging), 1936 (sleeping pills with wife)
- **Chronic chest problems:** Documented reason for draft exemption

## Literary Periods and Characteristics

**Early career (1933–1935):** Autobiographical experimentation; I-novel form; grim psychological narratives exploring isolation and debauchery

**Wartime years (1941–1945):** Retellings of classical Japanese stories; censorship constraints; escapist literary focus (Ihara Saikaku adaptations)

**Postwar period (1946–1948):** Peak popularity; dissolution and decline themes; controversial memoir incorporating Communist beliefs; final masterworks

## Influences and Literary Lineage

**Direct mentors:** Masuji Ibuse (established writer whose connections enabled Dazai's publication)

**Literary influences:** Ryūnosuke Akutagawa (idol whose suicide profoundly affected him), Murasaki Shikibu, Fyodor Dostoevsky, Ihara Saikaku (classical reteller model)

**Movements:** I-Novel tradition; *Buraiha* (decadent literary school)

## Literary Legacy and Reception

- *The Setting Sun* and *No Longer Human* established as modern Japanese classics
- *No Longer Human* remains "his most popular work outside of Japan"
- Modern Japanese writers cite *No Longer Human* as favorite or major influence
- Work characterized by confessional, psychologically introspective style
- Themes of alienation, social disqualification, and self-destruction remain resonant
- Adapted extensively: anime (*Aoi Bungaku*), manga, musicals, film (*Ningen Shikkaku: Osamu Dazai and the Three Women*, 2019)
- Dazai Osamu Prize established in his name

## Personal Temperament and Behavioral Descriptions

"A bohemian and an alcoholic" in postwar period. Yukio Mishima recalled disgust: "First, I dislike his face. Second, I dislike his rustic preference for urban sophistication. Third, I dislike the fact that he played roles that were not appropriate for him."

Described as engaging in "clowning"; disputed with Mishima over writer Ōgai Mori. Self-destructive patterns included financial excess on "clothes, alcohol, and prostitutes." Grave positioned facing Mori Ōgai's tomb, reflecting his respect for classical tradition despite personal dissolution.
